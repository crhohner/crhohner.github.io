<h1>CS 2112</h1>
Way, way back in 2022, I made the somewhat questionable decision to take CS2112:
Honors Object-Oriented Design and Data Structures. The class is infamous among Cornell
CS majors for its punishing workload. While that may be true, I'd be lying if I said
I regret taking the class.

<h1>Critter World</h1>
For our final project, we were tasked with creating a simulated ecosystem in which
"critters" could interact with each other and a simple, hexagon-based environment.
The critters were meant to be generic stand-ins for any plant or animal species,
but most groups chose to represent them as bugs anyway. As you may have guessed,
we went with a butterfly theme for our frontend.

Critters could move, take in information about their environments, attack,
reproduce sexually or asexually, eat, give up food, grow, and wait (which is the equivalent
of photosynthesis, as a critter which waits produces energy without consuming food).

As a side note, critters could sense their environment in a couple of ways which were analogous to sight and smell.
The smell sense reported the direction the critter should move in to reach food the fastest. This sense was based on
an implementation of Djikstra's Path-Finding Algorithm.

Not all critters are made the same. All critters are "born" with a set of instructions for how
they should act in a given time step, written in a custom scripting language dubbed "critter code." In each time step,
a critter would run through each of its rules until one of them produced an action, or it reached the last one, in which case
it would wait for that turn.

For example, you could make a "plant" critter giving it a script that tells it to do nothing but wait.
This critter would passively create energy for itself and never move. Beyond differences in the critter code, each critter had a set of physical attributes including its
defense, attack, health, and size.

I mentioned earlier that critters could reproduce. They could do this in two ways: sexually and asexually.
In the former case, the offspring would have a random combination of the parents' rules, creating a new script.
For the biology nerds out there, think of each line in the code, or rule, as a heritable trait.

In both cases, the offspring had the potential to be born with a mutation which would also be heritable in later generations.
A large part of the project involved figuring out how to parse the critter code into an abstract
syntax tree (AST) which could be manipulated to produce new "genomes" through mutation. The mutations included inserting random
expressions into the critter code, swapping expressions, transforming one type of expression into another, removing expressions,
and mixing up the order of rules.

The presence of heritable traits that lead to differential survival among the critters matches
nearly all of the key points of Darwin's Theory of Evolution. Perhaps the most fascinating
aspect of our critters was their ability to evolve. We didn't frequently leave large worlds running long enough to produce many traits that
could really be considered adaptations, but it was good fun just watching the gene pool slowly shift across generations.

<h1>The Ultimate Critter</h1>

During finals, our course staff hosted a fun event in which we could enter custom critters in various competitions. Unfortunately, my partners
were swamped with preparing for their other finals at the time, but I figured I'd still give it a go.
This was the ultimate test of my understanding of critter code. For fear of violating academic integrity,
I'm not going to give an example of critter code here, but I will describe some of the strategies I came up with
in my many rounds of testing my ultimate critter.

Critters can sense which species another critter they encounter belongs to. I was able to exploit this by writing
rules that instructed my critter to immediately attack critters from other species, which in this case meant from other teams.
Energy conservation is the single most important factor in winning these matches. Many teams had their
critters die right away as they expended too much energy growing or trying to mate. However, a critter that is too small
will be too weak to survive for very long. I found that mid-sized critters that prioritized path-finding to food could survive the best.

Our team ended up winning the Bread First Tournament, in which critters competed to collect the most food.
We won by default, as ours was the only critter to survive for long enough to collect any significant amount of food.

We also won the award for the best-looking UI design. During that finals week, I pulled an
all-nighter iterating over the graphics used in our UI in Procreate. I learned
a valuable lesson about the importance of contrast before settling on the butterfly theme.

As a fun little added bonus, I took advantage of the code that assigned colors to different species and applied some color theory
to make butterflies with two-toned wings. And, as a final detail, I implemented "Staff Mode," which turned each species into
a photo of a member of our beloved course staff.

<h1>Lessons Learned</h1>
The final project wasn't just a test of our coding abilities, but our abilities to
collaborate to produce clean, well-documented work. We learned how to write design
documents, document our code, implement the Model-View-Controller and Factory design
patterns, use git, and compromise and support each other as teammates.

I learned a valuable lesson about paying close attention to specifications. On two occasions we misread
or misinterpreted our assignment descriptions, leading to code that
was either way more convoluted than it needed to be, or just plain wrong. For example,
our AST was not entirely abstract in its first iteration.

Looking back on it all, I'd say taking CS 2112 was worth it. I was able to engage with the course material on
a deeper level than I would have been able to in the non-honors course,
and, beyond that, I learned how to execute a project from start to finish within a group. I
came out of the course having formed friendships with some of the
most hilarious, talented, and passionate students at Cornell.
