Penny, named after the Odessey's queen of Ithaca, is a 27 inch remote-controlled sailboat assembled from a kit by <a href="https://tippecanoeboats.com/t27">
Tippecanoe Boats. </a>
My roommate and I began building Penny in the summer of 2023. The two of us had our hands full with summer courses, but decided we wanted to put them to work on a project in our downtime.

Coming out of my first year of doing embedded systems programming on Cornell Unmanned Air Systems, I was looking to work on a project that would familiarize with remote-control hardware.

I already had some experience with woodworking going into this project, having spent the previous summer building an accoustic guitar at the <a href = "https://www.specimenproducts.com/chicago-school-of-guitar-making/" >Chicago School of Guitar Making. </a>
I also knew a thing or two about small sailboats. I'm not the world's greatest sailor, but I can make it across a lake and back without sinking.

Thanks to the Cornell Maker Club, we were able to set ourselves up in our school's Maker Space, complete with every tool we could possibly need to assemble our boat (and assemble her safely).

We began by assembling Penny's deck, hull, keel, and rudder. After attatching the keel to the hull, we realized that Penny could no longer lay flat on a table, so we built her a custom stand as well.

We just finished the first stages of rigging Penny, in which we inserted small rings into her deck and mast in place of cleats, attatched sidestays and forestay to her mast, and created a mast step to hold everything in place. My roommate even taught me how to use a hand drill!

Penny has a long ways to go until her maiden voyage, but we are just about ready to set up our two servos and reciever on our electronics platform.
After the electronics are finished, we will attatch the deck, clean everything up for varnish and paint, and finish rigging. More updates to come!
